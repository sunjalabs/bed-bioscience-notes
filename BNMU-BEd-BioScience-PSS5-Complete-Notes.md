# UNIT I — Nature of Science, Objectives, Lesson Planning, Teaching Aids

---

## 1.1 What is Science? Meaning and characteristics

**Meaning.** The word *Science* comes from the Latin word *scientia*, which means "to know" or "knowing". Before the 18th century science was called *natural philosophy*. Science is not only observation, experiment and analysis; it is also a way of life and an ever-expanding body of knowledge built through a process of inquiry.

According to **Fitzpatrick**, "Science is a cumulative and endless series of empirical observations which result in the formation of concepts and theories, with both concepts and theories being subject to modification in the light of further empirical observations."

In simple words, **the way to learn science is to do science.** It always tests ideas against evidence collected from the world around us.

**Important characteristics of Science:**

1. Science deals with the **natural world**, not supernatural myths. For example, it explains the growth of plants and the characteristics of animals.
2. Science is not merely a collection of facts; it tries to **understand happenings through analysis, testing and verification**.
3. Scientific knowledge is **based on evidence (empirical)**. Every claim must be supported by observation or experiment.
4. Science is **dynamic and tentative**: knowledge is long lasting yet open to change when new evidence appears.
5. Science is **objective**: it tries to remove personal bias.
6. Science is **systematic and organised**.
7. Science is **self-correcting**: it uses scepticism, peer review and replication.
8. Science is a **creative human endeavour** influenced by social and historical factors.
9. Science and technology are **not the same**, but they affect each other.

## 1.2 PYQ ANSWER — "Describe the philosophy and nature of Science" (Group I, Q1)

**Introduction.** Science is both a body of knowledge and a way of thinking about the world. To understand science fully we must look at two things: its **nature** (what science is and how it behaves) and its **philosophy** (the values and the way of thinking behind it).

**A. The Nature of Science.** The nature of science can be understood through three faces:

1. **Science as a systematic body of knowledge.** This body includes facts, concepts and theories. These are subject to error and change. Science is dynamic, and scientific information is constantly being rearranged and reorganised in the light of new knowledge.

2. **Science as a way of investigating.** Scientists approach the solution of any problem in an organised manner using observation, imagination and insight. The scientific method (observation, hypothesis, experiment, conclusion) is central here.

3. **Science as a way of thinking.** This produces the **scientific attitude**. A person with a scientific attitude shows:
   - Open-mindedness
   - Objectivity
   - Freedom from superstition
   - Belief in cause and effect relationships
   - Accuracy and truthfulness in reporting observations
   - A methodical way of solving problems
   - Up-to-dateness

**Key aspects of the nature of science:**
- Scientific knowledge is long lasting yet tentative.
- Empirical evidence is used to refine and support ideas.
- Social and historical factors play a role in building scientific knowledge.
- Laws and theories both matter but have different functions.
- Accurate record keeping, peer review and replication validate science.
- Science is a creative endeavour.
- Science and technology influence each other but are different.

**B. The Philosophy of Science.** The philosophy of science studies how scientific knowledge is created and what values guide it. It promotes **scepticism**: scientists view every new claim with suspicion, and a new observation is accepted only after it has been checked by independent individuals or groups giving identical results. Science rests on at least **four fundamental values**:
- Curiosity is good and should be encouraged.
- Knowledge itself is good; it is good to acquire knowledge.
- It is wrong to falsify or fabricate the data on which knowledge is based.
- It is good to keep an open mind (willing to examine new evidence) tempered by vigilant scepticism.

**Conclusion.** The nature of science tells us that science is dynamic, evidence-based, self-correcting and creative, while the philosophy of science tells us that honesty, curiosity, open-mindedness and scepticism are its guiding values. A science teacher must convey both the knowledge of science and this way of thinking to students.

## 1.3 Meaning, nature and scope of Biological Science (guide question)

**Meaning of Biological Science.** Biology is a natural science concerned with the study of life and living organisms, including their structure, function, growth, evolution, distribution, identification and taxonomy. The word "Biology" literally means **"the study of life"** (Greek *bios* = life, *logos* = study). It is a broad field covering everything from the minute working of chemical machines inside our cells to large-scale concepts like ecosystems and global climate change.

**Nature and Scope.**
- Biology has characteristics that mark it off from other spheres of human activity. Humans have always been curious about the living world.
- It is a **social endeavour**: knowledge becomes power and wisdom.
- Biology has the potential to be beneficial or harmful (the twentieth century shows both).
- In a progressive society biology plays a **liberating role**, freeing people from ignorance and superstition.
- The scope of biology includes botany, zoology, microbiology, genetics, ecology, physiology, biotechnology, medicine, agriculture and environmental science.

## 1.4 Aims and Objectives of Teaching Biological Science (guide question)

**Aims** are broad, long-term goals. **Objectives** are specific, measurable and achievable in one or a few lessons.

**Aims of teaching biological science in schools:**
1. To help students become **responsible democratic citizens**.
2. To make students appreciate how science and technology develop and affect diverse individuals, cultures and societies.
3. To encourage the **responsible use of science and technology** for the benefit of society.
4. To develop **critical thinking** about the interconnection of science, technology and society (STS).
5. To develop a **scientific attitude and scientific vision** so that students make rational choices in later life.
6. To develop skills of acquiring and processing information.
7. To build sensitivity towards a healthy and sustainable society and the environment.

**Objectives (Bloom's domains):**
- **Cognitive:** knowledge, comprehension, application, analysis, synthesis, evaluation of biological facts and concepts.
- **Affective:** developing interest, attitudes, values and appreciation for living things and nature.
- **Psychomotor:** developing skills of observation, drawing, dissection, handling apparatus and experimentation.

## 1.5 Objectives of teaching and writing behavioural (instructional) objectives

**Behavioural objective:** an objective written in terms of the observable behaviour the learner will be able to show after instruction. It uses **action verbs** (define, list, label, explain, draw, classify, compare, solve), not vague words like "know" or "understand".

**Format:** Condition + Action verb + Content + Standard.
Example: "After the lesson, the student will be able to **label** at least 5 parts of a prokaryotic cell on a diagram."

**Bloom's Taxonomy verbs used to write them:**
- Knowledge: define, list, name, state, recall.
- Comprehension: explain, describe, summarise.
- Application: solve, demonstrate, use, illustrate.
- Analysis: compare, differentiate, classify.
- Synthesis: design, construct, prepare.
- Evaluation: judge, justify, criticise.

## 1.6 PYQ ANSWER — "Describe the different steps of a Lesson Plan" (Group I, Q2)

**Definition.** A lesson plan is a **written description of the teaching and learning activities** that a teacher will carry out in a single class period to achieve specific objectives. It is the teacher's "blueprint" for one lesson. The most widely used model in B.Ed is the **Herbartian Five Steps** (given by J.F. Herbart and his followers).

**Preliminary information written on top of every lesson plan:**
- General information: name of school, class, subject, topic, date, period, duration.
- General objectives and specific (behavioural) objectives.
- Teaching aids and instructional materials.
- Previous knowledge assumed of the pupils.

**The main steps of a lesson plan (Herbartian steps):**

1. **Introduction / Motivation (Preparation).** The teacher tests the **previous knowledge** of students with a few introductory questions and creates interest, then announces the **statement of the aim** (the topic of the day). This links new learning to what the pupils already know.

2. **Presentation.** The heart of the lesson. The new content is taught step by step through **teaching points**, developing questions, explanation, demonstration and use of teaching aids. The teacher and pupils develop the lesson together (this is shown in a two-column format: Teaching Points and Teacher-Pupil Activity).

3. **Comparison and Association.** New facts are **compared and linked** with related facts or examples already known, so that ideas are organised and similarities and differences become clear.

4. **Generalisation.** From the facts presented, the class arrives at a **general rule, principle, definition or conclusion.** This should come from the pupils as far as possible, not be dictated by the teacher.

5. **Application.** The generalisation is **applied to new situations or problems** to check understanding and to make the knowledge functional and lasting.

6. **Recapitulation / Review and Evaluation.** The teacher asks **review questions** to assess whether the objectives were achieved, corrects errors, and finally gives **home assignment / home work.**

**Importance of a lesson plan:** gives direction and confidence to the teacher, ensures proper use of time, keeps the lesson organised and goal-oriented, helps proper use of teaching aids, and provides a record for evaluation and improvement.

## 1.7 Instructional materials and teaching aids (supporting topic for Unit I)

**Teaching aids** are tools that make teaching effective, clear and interesting. They follow the principle: *the more senses involved, the better the learning* (the "cone of experience" idea of Edgar Dale).

**Classification:**
- **Audio aids:** radio, audio recordings, tape recorder.
- **Visual aids:** chart, model, blackboard, picture, specimen, chalkboard, OHP, improvised apparatus, herbarium, aquarium.
- **Audio-visual aids:** television, film, computer, multimedia, LCD projector, video.

**Components of instructional materials:** multimedia, computer, charts, models, improvised apparatus, and prepared unit plans and lesson plans.

**Qualities of a good teaching aid:** simple, accurate, large enough to be seen, relevant to the topic, low cost, easy to handle.

**Improvised apparatus:** apparatus made from cheap, locally available material (bottles, straws, cardboard) when standard apparatus is not available. It encourages creativity and is stressed in activity-based learning.

## 1.8 Constructivism and NCF-2005 (Unit I version; the full NCF answer is in Unit V, section 5.6)

**Constructivism** is the view that learners **actively build (construct) their own knowledge** by connecting new experiences to their previous knowledge, rather than passively receiving facts. The teacher is a **facilitator**, not just a transmitter of information. NCF-2005 strongly recommends the constructivist approach: learning by doing, activity-based teaching, and connecting knowledge to life outside school.

## 2.2 PYQ ANSWER — "Describe the Heuristic method of teaching Biological Science" (Group II, Q3)

**Meaning.** The word *Heuristic* comes from the Greek word *heurisko*, which means **"I find out"** or **"I discover".** In this method the learner is placed in the position of a **discoverer or investigator** and learns by **finding out things for himself** instead of being told. The method was developed and popularised by **Prof. Henry Edward Armstrong**.

**Central principle.** *"The way to learn science is to do science."* The teacher gives the minimum guidance and the student is made to act like a small scientist: observing, experimenting, reasoning and drawing conclusions on his own.

**Role of the teacher.** The teacher is only a **guide and friend.** He sets the problem, provides the materials and direction, and asks leading questions, but he does **not give the answer.** The student works, makes mistakes, and discovers the truth himself.

**Steps in the heuristic method:**
1. The teacher presents a **problem** to the class.
2. Students are given **materials and apparatus** to work with.
3. Students **observe and experiment** to gather facts.
4. Students **record their observations.**
5. Students **reason out and draw conclusions / generalise** from their data.
6. The conclusion is **verified and applied.**

**Example in Biology.** Instead of telling students "leaves give out oxygen", the teacher gives them a water plant (Hydrilla), a beaker, water and a funnel, and asks them to find out which gas is released in sunlight. The students set up the experiment, collect the gas, test it, and **discover for themselves** that oxygen is given out during photosynthesis.

**Merits (advantages):**
- Develops the **scientific attitude and habit of inquiry.**
- Knowledge gained is **permanent** because the student discovers it himself.
- Develops **self-reliance, observation, reasoning and experimental skills.**
- Makes learning **active and interesting.**
- Trains students in the **scientific method.**

**Demerits (limitations):**
- **Very slow** and time-consuming; little content is covered.
- Not suitable for **young or weak students**, who need guidance.
- Demands a **highly skilled teacher** and good laboratory facilities.
- Not suitable for the whole syllabus or for large classes.
- Expects every child to be a discoverer, which is not always practical.

**Conclusion.** The heuristic method is excellent for **developing scientific attitude and process skills**, but because it is slow it is best used in combination with other methods rather than alone.

## 2.3 Other methods in brief (in case a different one is asked)

**Project method (Kilpatrick).** Learning by doing a purposeful real-life task. Steps: providing a situation, choosing the project, planning, executing, evaluating, recording. Example: prepare a school kitchen garden or a herbarium.

**Problem solving method.** Students solve a real problem using scientific steps: recognising the problem, defining it, forming hypotheses, collecting data, testing, and concluding.

**Demonstration method.** The teacher performs an experiment while students observe. Good when apparatus is costly or risky. It is economical of time and material but pupils remain mostly passive.

**Inductive method.** Moves from **particular examples to a general rule** (specific to general). Good for forming concepts and laws.

**Deductive method.** Moves from the **general rule to particular examples** (general to specific). Quick but does not build understanding by itself. Inductive and deductive are usually used together.

**Lecture method.** Teacher-centred verbal teaching. Covers much content quickly but makes pupils passive; improved by combining with demonstration (lecture-cum-demonstration).

**Programmed instruction.** Self-instructional material broken into small steps (frames); the learner responds and gets immediate feedback. Based on Skinner's operant conditioning. Linear and branching types.

## 2.4 Evaluation, types of test items, and types of tests

**Evaluation** is the process of judging how far learning objectives have been achieved. It can be:
- **Oral** (questioning, observation) and **written.**
- **Formative** (during teaching) and **summative** (at the end).

**Types of test questions:**
- **Essay type:** long answers; test expression and organisation; but subjective and hard to mark uniformly.
- **Short answer type:** answered in a few sentences; cover more content with more objectivity.
- **Objective type:** single correct answer, fully objective. Forms: multiple choice, fill in the blanks, true-false, matching.

**Types of tests:**
- **Achievement test:** measures how much a student has learnt after teaching.
- **Diagnostic test:** finds the specific weaknesses and learning difficulties of a student so that remedial teaching can be planned.

## 3.1 PYQ ANSWER — "What is Mitosis? Describe its various stages" (Group III, Q5)

**Definition.** **Mitosis** is a type of cell division in which a single parent cell divides to produce **two daughter cells**, each having the **same number of chromosomes** and identical genetic material as the parent. It occurs in the **somatic (body) cells** and is also called **equational division.** It is responsible for growth, repair and replacement of cells.

**Before mitosis — Interphase.** The cell first passes through interphase (G1, S, G2). During the **S phase the DNA is replicated**, so each chromosome now has two sister chromatids. Interphase is preparation; the actual division (karyokinesis) has four stages, remembered as **P-M-A-T.**

**Stages of mitosis:**

1. **Prophase.**
   - Chromatin threads condense into distinct **chromosomes**, each made of two sister chromatids joined at the **centromere.**
   - The **nuclear membrane and nucleolus begin to disappear.**
   - In animal cells the **centrioles move to opposite poles** and the **spindle fibres** start to form.

2. **Metaphase.**
   - Chromosomes arrange themselves at the **equator (metaphase plate)** of the cell.
   - Spindle fibres attach to the centromere of each chromosome.
   - This is the stage where chromosomes are most clearly visible.

3. **Anaphase.**
   - The **centromeres split** and the two sister chromatids of each chromosome **separate.**
   - The separated chromatids (now daughter chromosomes) **move to opposite poles** pulled by the spindle fibres.

4. **Telophase.**
   - Chromosomes reach the poles and **uncoil back into chromatin.**
   - The **nuclear membrane and nucleolus reappear** around each set.
   - The spindle fibres disappear. Two nuclei are now formed.

**Cytokinesis.** Division of the cytoplasm. In **animal cells** it happens by a **cleavage furrow** that pinches the cell in two; in **plant cells** a **cell plate** forms in the middle and grows into a new cell wall. The result is **two identical diploid daughter cells.**

**Significance of mitosis:** growth of the body, repair and healing of wounds, replacement of dead cells, and asexual reproduction in some organisms. It keeps the chromosome number constant generation after generation.

**Quick diagram cue (draw these four circles in your answer):** show a round cell for each stage with chromosomes: scattered (prophase), lined up at the centre (metaphase), pulled apart to two sides (anaphase), two nuclei forming (telophase).

## 3.2 Meiosis (for comparison; sometimes asked)

**Meiosis** is the division of **reproductive (germ) cells** that produces **four daughter cells**, each with **half (haploid) the chromosome number** of the parent. It is also called **reductional division.** It involves **two divisions, Meiosis I and Meiosis II.**

- **Meiosis I (reductional):** homologous chromosomes pair, **crossing over** occurs (exchange of segments, the source of variation), and then the homologous chromosomes separate. Prophase I has sub-stages: leptotene, zygotene, pachytene (crossing over), diplotene, diakinesis.
- **Meiosis II (equational):** similar to mitosis; sister chromatids separate.

**Importance:** keeps the chromosome number constant across generations (because fertilisation later doubles it back), and produces **genetic variation** through crossing over and independent assortment.

**Mitosis vs Meiosis (key differences):**

| Feature | Mitosis | Meiosis |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Cells where it occurs | Body (somatic) cells | Germ (reproductive) cells |
| Number of divisions | One | Two |
| Daughter cells | 2 | 4 |
| Chromosome number | Same as parent (diploid) | Half of parent (haploid) |
| Crossing over | Absent | Present |
| Purpose | Growth, repair | Gamete formation, variation |

## 3.3 PYQ ANSWER — "What is Balanced Diet? Explain any two deficiency diseases" (Group III, Q6)

**Definition.** A **balanced diet** is a diet that contains **all the essential nutrients** (carbohydrates, proteins, fats, vitamins, minerals, water and roughage) in the **correct proportion and right amount** required by the body for proper growth, energy, maintenance and good health.

**Components of a balanced diet and their functions:**

| Nutrient | Main function | Sources |
|----------|---------------|---------|
| Carbohydrates | Provide energy | Rice, wheat, potato, sugar |
| Proteins | Growth and body building, repair | Pulses, milk, egg, fish, meat |
| Fats | Concentrated energy, store | Oil, ghee, butter, nuts |
| Vitamins | Protective, regulate body functions | Fruits, vegetables |
| Minerals | Build bones, teeth, blood; regulate | Milk, green vegetables, salt |
| Water | Medium for all reactions, transport | Water, fruits, drinks |
| Roughage (fibre) | Helps in digestion, clears bowels | Salad, whole grains, vegetables |

A diet is "balanced" only when these are present in the right proportion according to a person's age, sex, work and climate.

**Deficiency diseases** are illnesses caused by the **lack of one or more nutrients** in the diet over a long time. Here are two explained in detail (write any two):

**1. Scurvy (deficiency of Vitamin C / ascorbic acid).**
- **Cause:** Long-term lack of Vitamin C, found in citrus fruits (orange, lemon, amla), tomato and green vegetables.
- **Symptoms:** Bleeding and swollen gums, loosening and falling of teeth, slow healing of wounds, weakness, pain in joints, anaemia.
- **Prevention / cure:** Eating fresh fruits rich in Vitamin C, especially citrus fruits and amla (Indian gooseberry).

**2. Rickets (deficiency of Vitamin D).**
- **Cause:** Lack of Vitamin D (and calcium). Vitamin D is made in the skin in sunlight and is found in milk, egg yolk, fish and butter.
- **Symptoms:** Soft and weak bones, bent or bow-shaped legs, deformed chest, delayed teeth in children. In adults the same deficiency causes **osteomalacia** (soft bones).
- **Prevention / cure:** Exposure to morning sunlight, and a diet with milk, eggs, fish and Vitamin D.

**Other common deficiency diseases (table for safety):**

| Deficient nutrient | Disease |
|--------------------|---------|
| Protein (in children) | Kwashiorkor, Marasmus |
| Vitamin A | Night blindness, Xerophthalmia |
| Vitamin B1 (thiamine) | Beriberi |
| Vitamin B3 (niacin) | Pellagra |
| Vitamin B12 / Iron | Anaemia |
| Iodine | Goitre |
| Calcium / Vitamin D | Rickets, Osteomalacia |
| Vitamin C | Scurvy |

## 3.4 Communicable and non-communicable diseases (supporting topic, Units III and V)

**Communicable (infectious) diseases** are caused by **pathogens** (bacteria, viruses, protozoa, fungi, worms) and **spread from one person to another** through air, water, food, contact or vectors (insects).
- Examples: Tuberculosis, Cholera, Typhoid, Malaria, Influenza, COVID-19, Hepatitis, Common cold.
- Symptoms (general): fever, cough, weakness, depending on the disease.
- Prevention and control: vaccination, cleanliness and sanitation, safe drinking water, killing vectors (mosquitoes), isolation of patients, health education.

**Non-communicable diseases** are **not spread** from person to person. They are caused by lifestyle, deficiency, genetics or organ malfunction.
- Examples: Diabetes, High blood pressure (hypertension), Heart disease, Cancer, deficiency diseases.
- Prevention and control: balanced diet, regular exercise, avoiding tobacco and alcohol, stress control, regular check-ups.

## 4.1 The Cell: prokaryotic and eukaryotic

The **cell** is the basic structural and functional unit of life (Cell Theory: Schleiden, Schwann, and Virchow). Cells are of two types:

| Feature | Prokaryotic cell | Eukaryotic cell |
|---------|------------------|-----------------|
| Nucleus | No true nucleus (nucleoid, no membrane) | True membrane-bound nucleus |
| Size | Small (1 to 10 micrometre) | Larger (10 to 100 micrometre) |
| Membrane-bound organelles | Absent | Present (mitochondria, ER, etc.) |
| Ribosomes | 70S | 80S |
| DNA | Single circular, naked | Linear, with proteins (chromosomes) |
| Examples | Bacteria, blue-green algae | Plant, animal, fungal cells |

## 4.2 PYQ ANSWER — "Draw a labelled diagram of a Prokaryotic cell" (Group IV, Q7)

## 4.4 PYQ ANSWER — "Describe the different types of Permanent tissues" (Group IV, Q8)

**Meaning.** **Permanent tissues** are tissues made of cells that have **lost the power of division** and have taken up a **definite shape, size and function.** They are formed from meristematic tissue (which is the dividing tissue, found at root tips, shoot tips, etc.). Permanent tissues are of two main types: **Simple** and **Complex** (plus special/secretory tissues).

**A. Simple permanent tissues** (made of only **one type of cell**):

1. **Parenchyma.**
   - Living cells, thin-walled, with intercellular spaces.
   - Functions: **storage** of food and water, photosynthesis (when it has chlorophyll, it is called **chlorenchyma**), and gives buoyancy (as **aerenchyma** in water plants).

2. **Collenchyma.**
   - Living cells, **thickened at the corners.**
   - Function: provides **flexible mechanical support** to young stems and leaves; allows bending without breaking.

3. **Sclerenchyma.**
   - **Dead cells with very thick, lignified walls** (no living content).
   - Function: gives **hardness, rigidity and strength**; found in husks, nut shells, and as fibres (jute, coconut).

**B. Complex permanent tissues** (made of **more than one type of cell** working together; these are the conducting/vascular tissues):

1. **Xylem.** Conducts **water and minerals** from the roots to the leaves and gives mechanical support. It is made of four elements: **tracheids, vessels, xylem parenchyma and xylem fibres.** Most xylem cells are dead.

2. **Phloem.** Conducts **food (prepared in leaves)** to all parts of the plant (translocation). It is made of four elements: **sieve tubes, companion cells, phloem parenchyma and phloem fibres.** Phloem is mostly living.

**C. Special / secretory tissues.** Some textbooks add these: **laticiferous tissue** (contains latex, as in rubber and papaya plants) and **glandular tissue** (secretes oils, resins, nectar, for example resin ducts, oil glands and nectaries).

**Summary cue:** Simple = one cell type (parenchyma, collenchyma, sclerenchyma). Complex = many cell types (xylem carries water, phloem carries food).

---

## 4.5 Meristematic tissue (supporting topic for Units III and IV)

**Meristematic tissue** is made of **young, dividing cells** that keep producing new cells for growth. It is the opposite of permanent tissue.
- **Apical meristem:** at the tips of roots and shoots; causes increase in **length.**
- **Lateral meristem (cambium):** at the sides; causes increase in **girth (thickness).**
- **Intercalary meristem:** at the base of leaves or internodes; helps in length growth, as in grasses.

**Epidermis** is the **outermost protective layer** of cells covering the plant body. It protects against water loss, injury and infection, and is often covered by a waxy **cuticle.** Stomata (with guard cells) in the epidermis allow gas exchange.

# UNIT V — Animal Tissues and NCF-2005

---

## 5.1 The four animal tissues at a glance

| Tissue | Main job | Examples / location |
|--------|----------|---------------------|
| Epithelial | Covering and lining, protection, secretion | Skin surface, lining of mouth, gut, blood vessels |
| Connective | Binding, support, transport | Blood, bone, cartilage, tendon, ligament, adipose |
| Muscular | Movement by contraction | Skeletal, smooth and cardiac muscle |
| Nervous | Control and coordination | Brain, spinal cord, nerves |

---

## 5.2 Epithelial tissue

**Structure:** A tissue of closely packed cells with little space between them, resting on a **basement membrane.** It forms the covering and lining of body surfaces and organs.

**Types:** squamous (flat, for example skin and lining of blood vessels), cuboidal (cube-shaped, in kidney tubules and glands), columnar (tall, in the gut for absorption), ciliated (with hair-like cilia, in the windpipe), and glandular (modified to secrete, as in glands).

**Functions:** protection, absorption, secretion, filtration and reduction of friction.

---

## 5.3 Connective tissue

**Structure:** The **most abundant** tissue; cells are **loosely spaced in a matrix** (the matrix may be jelly-like, fluid or solid).

**Types and functions:**
- **Areolar tissue:** fills spaces, supports organs.
- **Adipose tissue:** stores fat, insulates the body.
- **Blood:** fluid connective tissue; transports oxygen, food, wastes and hormones.
- **Bone:** hard, gives the body its framework and support.
- **Cartilage:** flexible, supports the nose, ear and joints.
- **Tendon:** joins muscle to bone; **Ligament:** joins bone to bone.

**Functions:** binding and connecting organs, support, transport (blood), storage (fat), defence.

---

## 5.4 Muscular tissue

**Structure:** Made of long contractile cells called **muscle fibres** that contain special proteins (actin and myosin) which allow them to contract and relax, producing movement.

**Three types:**
- **Striated / skeletal muscle:** has stripes, **voluntary** (under our control), attached to bones, helps in body movement.
- **Smooth / unstriated muscle:** no stripes, **involuntary**, found in the walls of the stomach, intestine and blood vessels.
- **Cardiac muscle:** found only in the heart wall; striated but **involuntary**, contracts rhythmically throughout life without tiring.

**Function:** movement of the body and of materials inside the body (food in the gut, blood in vessels).

## 5.5 PYQ ANSWER — "Explain the structure and function of nervous tissue" (Group V, Q9)

**Meaning.** **Nervous tissue** is the tissue that **receives stimuli and conducts impulses** from one part of the body to another, providing **control and coordination.** It makes up the brain, spinal cord and nerves. It is built of two kinds of cells: **neurons (nerve cells)**, which actually carry impulses, and **neuroglia (glial cells)**, which support and nourish the neurons.

**Structure of a neuron (the functional unit):**
1. **Cell body (cyton / soma):** contains the **nucleus** and cytoplasm with granules called **Nissl granules.** It controls the activity of the neuron.
2. **Dendrites:** short, branched extensions that **receive impulses** and carry them towards the cell body.
3. **Axon:** a single long fibre that **carries the impulse away** from the cell body to the next cell.
4. **Myelin sheath:** a fatty insulating cover around the axon that speeds up the impulse; the gaps in it are the **Nodes of Ranvier.**
5. **Axon endings (synaptic knobs):** the tips of the axon that pass the impulse to the next neuron or to a muscle across a junction called the **synapse**, using chemicals called **neurotransmitters.**

**Types of neurons (by function):**
- **Sensory (afferent) neurons:** carry impulses **from sense organs to the brain / spinal cord.**
- **Motor (efferent) neurons:** carry impulses **from the brain / spinal cord to muscles and glands.**
- **Interneurons (association neurons):** connect sensory and motor neurons.

**Neuroglia:** supporting cells (astrocytes, oligodendrocytes, microglia, Schwann cells) that hold neurons in place, nourish them, insulate them and remove waste. They do **not** conduct impulses.

**Functions of nervous tissue:**
1. **Receives stimuli** from inside and outside the body.
2. **Conducts nerve impulses** rapidly from one part to another.
3. Provides **coordination and control** of all body activities.
4. Brings about a quick **response** (including reflex actions).
5. Is the basis of **thinking, memory and the senses.**

**Diagram cue:** draw a neuron with a star-shaped cell body on the left (with nucleus), several short dendrites branching from it, one long axon to the right covered by oval myelin segments with gaps (nodes of Ranvier), ending in small branched knobs.

## 5.6 PYQ ANSWER — "Describe the main points of NCF-2005 in light of Science" (Group V, Q10)

**What NCF-2005 is.** The **National Curriculum Framework 2005** was prepared by the **NCERT.** It is a guiding document for school education in India. Its most famous theme is: **"connect knowledge to life outside the school"** and to move away from **rote (memory-based) learning** towards **understanding and learning by doing.**

**The five guiding principles of NCF-2005:**
1. Connecting knowledge to **life outside the school.**
2. Shifting from **rote methods** of learning to understanding.
3. Enriching the curriculum so that it **goes beyond textbooks.**
4. Making **examinations more flexible** and integrating them with classroom life.
5. Nurturing an **identity informed by caring concerns** within the democratic framework of the Constitution.

**NCF-2005 in light of Science (the main points for science education):**

1. **Constructivist approach:** the child **constructs his own knowledge** through activity; the teacher is a facilitator, not a lecturer.
2. **Learning by doing / activity-based learning:** science should be taught through experiments, observation, projects and hands-on activity, not memorising. (The syllabus notes that Units III, IV and V are to be taught with this activity-based emphasis.)
3. **Child-centred teaching:** teaching starts from the child's own experience and environment.
4. **Relating science to daily life and environment.**
5. **Developing scientific attitude, process skills and problem-solving ability** rather than only facts.
6. **Reducing curriculum load** and avoiding rote learning ("learning without burden").
7. **Inquiry and discovery** are encouraged (this links to the heuristic and project methods).
8. **Examination reform:** continuous and comprehensive evaluation instead of one final memory test.

**Six criteria of validity for a good science curriculum (given by NCF-2005):**

| Criterion | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| **Cognitive validity** | Content and method should suit the age and mental level of the child |
| **Content validity** | The content should be correct and significant |
| **Process validity** | Children should learn the **process** of doing science (the method), not only conclusions |
| **Historical validity** | Science should be shown as an evolving, human, social endeavour |
| **Environmental validity** | Science should be placed in the context of the child's environment and local issues |
| **Ethical validity** | Science teaching should build honesty, objectivity and concern for life and environment |

**Conclusion.** In the light of science, NCF-2005 wants a science classroom that is **activity-based, child-centred, constructivist and connected to real life**, developing scientific attitude and process skills rather than rote memory.
